🗞️ What Makes a Great Newspaper Layout?

Real newspapers use specific design features to grab your attention and make articles easy to read. When you create a newspaper in Word, you need to think about:

The Masthead This is the big, fancy title at the top of the newspaper (like "The Times" or "The Guardian"). In Word, you create this using WordArt — a tool that lets you make decorative, eye-catching text.
Headlines Headlines tell the reader what the article is about. They need to be big (large font size), bold, and sometimes underlined. You use the Home tab in Word for all of this.
Columns Real newspapers use columns to split text into two or three sections side by side. This makes long articles much easier to read. In Word, you find columns under the Layout tab.

Lesson 1 — Step by Step

WordArt, Headlines & Columns

Follow these instructions carefully to set up your newspaper in Word.

🎨 Skill A: Creating a WordArt Masthead

The masthead is the title of your newspaper. We use WordArt to make it look professional and eye-catching.

📘 Microsoft Word — Insert Tab
📷 Pictures 📊 Table 🎨 WordArt 📐 Shapes
THE ACADEMY HERALD
Your WordArt masthead will look something like this
1
Open Microsoft Word

Open a new blank document. Make sure you can see the ribbon at the top.

2
Click the Insert tab

Look at the tabs along the top of Word. Click Insert (it's between Home and Design).

3
Click WordArt

In the Insert tab, find the WordArt button (it has a big letter "A" with effects). Click it and choose a style — pick something bold and professional (avoid rainbow colours!).

4
Type your newspaper name

A text box will appear. Delete the placeholder text and type your newspaper name, e.g. "The Academy Herald" or "School Times".

5
Centre it and resize

Click on the WordArt, then press Ctrl + E to centre it. Drag the corner handles to make it bigger or smaller.

Pro Tip: Changing WordArt Style Click on your WordArt, then look for the Format tab that appears. Here you can change the colour, outline, shadow, and text effects.

📝 Skill B: Creating a Bold Headline

Every newspaper article needs a headline that grabs the reader's attention. Here's how to format one properly.

1
Press Enter after your WordArt

Click below the WordArt masthead and press Enter to start a new line.

2
Type your headline

Type a short, attention-grabbing headline. Good headlines are usually 6-10 words. For example: "Sports Day Smashes Records as Year 9 Dominate"

3
Select the headline text

Click and drag to highlight all the headline text, or use Ctrl + A if it's the only text.

4
Make it bold and increase the font size

Press Ctrl + B to make it bold. Then change the font size to 22-28pt using the font size box on the Home tab.

5
(Optional) Underline it

If you want, press Ctrl + U to underline your headline. This can help it stand out even more.

Common Mistake! Don't make your headline ALL CAPS by holding Shift. Instead, type normally and use Bold and a large font size. ALL CAPS headlines look like you're shouting!

📊 Skill C: Setting Up 2-Column Layout

Columns make your newspaper look professional. Here's how to split your article text into two columns.

📘 Microsoft Word — Layout Tab
📐 Margins 📏 Size 📊 Columns ↕️ Breaks
Last Friday saw the school's annual Sports Day deliver one of its most exciting competitions in years. Year 9 student Jake Thompson stormed to victory in the 100m sprint, clocking an impressive 12.3 seconds.

The long jump and shot put events were equally competitive, with records broken in both categories. Staff even took part in a relay race, much to the delight of the watching students.
1
Type your article text first

Press Enter after your headline and type (or paste) your article text. Write at least 2-3 paragraphs.

2
Select the article text

Highlight only the article text (not the masthead or headline). Click at the start and drag to the end.

3
Go to Layout tab > Columns

Click the Layout tab at the top, then click Columns. Choose "Two" from the dropdown.

4
Check it looks right

Your text should now flow in two columns side by side. If the whole page changed (including your heading), press Ctrl + Z to undo and try again — make sure you only selected the article text.

Keyboard Shortcuts Recap Ctrl + B = Bold  •  Ctrl + U = Underline  •  Ctrl + E = Centre text  •  Ctrl + Z = Undo

Images, Tables & Finishing

Make your newspaper look amazing with images, text wrapping, and a professional events table.

1 Insert and resize images in your newspaper

3 Insert a table for a school events calendar

🖼️ Why Do Newspapers Use Images?

Images are one of the most important parts of a newspaper. They:

Grab Attention A good photo makes people stop and read the article. Without images, newspapers would be boring walls of text that nobody wants to read.
Tell the Story A picture is worth a thousand words! An image of Sports Day shows the action better than any amount of text.
Text Wrapping is Key! In Word, when you insert an image, it usually just sits on its own line. To make it look like a real newspaper, you need to set text wrapping so the article text flows around the image. The two best options are Square and Tight.

📊 Why Do Newspapers Use Tables?

Tables are perfect for organising information that has multiple pieces of data. In a newspaper, tables are great for:

  • Events calendars — What, When, Where
  • Sports results — Teams, Scores, Dates
  • Timetables — Days, Times, Activities
  • League tables — Position, Team, Points

Images, Text Wrapping & Tables

Follow these instructions to add images and a table to your newspaper.

🖼️ Skill A: Inserting & Resizing Images

Your newspaper needs at least 2 images. Here's how to insert them and make them the right size.

📘 Microsoft Word — Insert Tab
📷 Pictures 🌐 Online Pictures 📊 Table 🎨 WordArt
Sports Day Results
📸 Image with
Square text wrap
Last Friday saw the school's annual Sports Day deliver one of its most exciting competitions in years. Year 9 student Jake Thompson stormed to victory in the 100m sprint. The crowd cheered as he crossed the line first.
1
Click where you want the image

Click in your article text roughly where you want the image to appear.

2
Go to Insert > Pictures

Click the Insert tab, then click Pictures. Choose "This Device" to find a picture saved on the computer, or "Online Pictures" to search the web.

3
Select your image and click Insert

Browse to find a suitable image, click it, then click Insert. The image will appear in your document.

4
Resize the image

Click on the image to see the resize handles (small circles in the corners). Drag a corner handle to make it bigger or smaller. Always use a corner — this keeps the proportions correct!

Important: Use Corner Handles Only! If you drag the handles on the sides (not corners), your image will get stretched and look wrong. Always drag from the corner to resize properly.

📐 Skill B: Setting Text Wrapping

By default, images in Word sit on their own line. To make text flow around the image (like a real newspaper), you need to change the text wrapping.

1
Click on the image

Click on the image you want to wrap text around. You'll see it get selected with handles.

2
Look for the Layout Options icon

When you click the image, you'll see a small icon appear at the top-right corner of the image. It looks like a tiny rainbow arc. Click it!

3
Choose "Square" or "Tight"

Square wraps text in a rectangle around the image. Tight wraps text closely to the shape of the image. Both work great for newspapers — Square is the easiest.

4
Drag the image into position

Now you can click and drag the image anywhere on the page. The text will flow around it automatically. Try placing one image on the right side of your article.

Alternative Method You can also right-click the image, choose "Wrap Text", and select Square or Tight from the menu.

📊 Skill C: Inserting a Table

Tables are perfect for showing events, results, or schedules in your newspaper. You need a table with 4 columns (Event, Date, Time, Location) and at least 4 rows.

📘 Microsoft Word — Insert > Table

1
Click below your article text

Position your cursor below the article where you want the table to appear. You might want to add a sub-heading first (e.g. "Upcoming Events").

2
Go to Insert > Table

Click the Insert tab, then click Table. A grid will appear — hover over it and select 4 columns x 5 rows (4 across, 5 down).

3
Type the column headings

Click in the first cell and type Event. Press Tab to move to the next cell. Type Date, Tab, Time, Tab, Location.

4
Fill in the rows with events

Press Tab to move to the next row and fill in your school events. Use real or made-up events!

5
Format the header row

Select the top row (click and drag across all 4 cells). Make it bold (Ctrl + B) and add a background colour using the Shading button (paint bucket icon) on the Table Design tab.

🔲 Skill D: Adding Borders

Borders make your table and your whole newspaper look neat and professional.

1
Select the whole table

Click the small crosshair icon at the top-left corner of the table to select the entire thing.

2
Find the Borders button

On the Table Design tab (which appears when you click in a table), look for the Borders button (it looks like a grid). Click the dropdown arrow next to it.

3
Choose "All Borders"

Select "All Borders" to add lines around every cell. This makes the table much easier to read.

1. Which text wrapping option makes text flow around an image?
Option A

In Line with Text

The image sits on its own line. Text appears above and below but NOT beside it.

Option B

Square

Text flows around the image in a rectangle shape. The image can be placed anywhere on the page.

Option A

Drag from a corner handle

This keeps the width and height in proportion, so the image doesn't get squashed or stretched.

Option B

Drag from a side handle

This only changes one dimension, making the image look stretched and distorted.
